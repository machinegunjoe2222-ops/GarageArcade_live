HOW TURBO CHARGERS WORK

Free power from exhaust gas

A turbocharger is, at its core, a simple idea: use exhaust gas that would otherwise go to waste and turn it into extra power. Exhaust leaving the engine still carries a huge amount of energy. Instead of letting that energy disappear out the tailpipe, a turbocharger captures it and puts it back to work.

The turbocharger has two halves connected by a shared shaft. On one side sits the turbine, spun by the rush of hot exhaust gas. On the other side sits the compressor, which draws in fresh air and pushes it into the engine under pressure. Because both wheels share a shaft, spinning the turbine automatically spins the compressor too, often at well over one hundred thousand revolutions per minute.

This pressurized air is what people mean by boost. Cramming more air into the cylinders means more oxygen is available for combustion, so the engine can burn more fuel per cycle and produce more power, all without increasing the engine's physical size.

Compressing air makes it hotter, and hot air is less dense, which works against the whole point of boost. That is why most turbocharged engines use an intercooler, a radiator-like unit that cools the pressurized air before it reaches the intake manifold. Cooler, denser air means more oxygen molecules packed into the same space, and a lower risk of the engine running too hot internally.

Boost pressure cannot simply climb forever. A wastegate is a valve that bleeds off some exhaust gas, bypassing the turbine when boost reaches its target level. This protects the engine and the turbocharger itself from being overwhelmed by too much pressure.

One well known characteristic of turbocharged engines is turbo lag, the brief delay between pressing the accelerator and feeling the full effect of boost. This happens because the turbine needs a moment to spin up to speed once exhaust flow increases. Smaller turbines spin up faster but run out of breath at high engine speeds, while larger turbines do the opposite, which is why some engines use two turbochargers of different sizes working together.

In the end, a turbocharger lets a smaller, lighter engine produce the power of a much larger one, simply by putting exhaust energy that used to go to waste back to work.
